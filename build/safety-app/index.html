Gendering Surveillance
 
 
 
 
 

Is Your Saviour Downloadable?

A startling number of apps seek to make public spaces safer for women. Safety apps are packaged as one of the top solutions to address the problem of high rates of crimes against women, by so many slants of actors- police forces of different states, government departments, political parties, “IIT students”, winners of industry contests on the theme of women’s safety, wearables start-ups, entertainment businesses, telecommunication service providers and companies concerned about the safety of their women employees.

A cursory search for ‘Nirbhaya’ on the Play Store will already list over a dozen apps- the search word referencing the rape that took place in public transport and led to an upswell of sentiment about crimes against women in the country. Many more apps can be discovered with a combination of some of these search terms: ‘women’, ‘safety’, ‘panic’, ‘button’, ‘sos’.

We assessed 50 women’s safety apps to see what features these apps offer, whether their commitment to women’s safety is reflected in their data policies and who is being excluded in these solutions.

At a glance...

  1. We identified the different types of features that make up these apps, and found that most of them don’t do much to increase autonomy of the user. Features like constant tracking and geofencing reduce autonomy of the user, making the user dependant on others to come to their rescue at best, and extending surveillance by family members at worst.

  1. We also identified different types of actors to whom the alert is sent to, like the police, or networked publics. It appears that the reliability of response in some of these options (like in case of alerts showing up on one’s Facebook feed) is quite dubious.

  1. Several privacy and security concerns emerged, as many of these apps don’t have a privacy policy, let alone policies making specific commitments about collection, storage and use of personal information. The apps that do have privacy policies are not transparent about if and how personal information is used or monetised. This raises questions about whether the understanding of safety as mere bodily harm is sufficient, and whether disembodied effects of collection and use of data by these apps is introducing fresh threats to safety.

  1. We found many accessibility issues for persons with visual impairment across the 50 apps.

Why are these apps so bad?

Most apps aimed at making public spaces safer are designed to do so by reducing the time taken to contact someone in a dangerous situation encountered. The latest data from National Crime Records Bureau shows that less than 6 % of rapes in India are stranger rapes. This trend has been common knowledge for a while now. And yet, the enthusiasm and self-congratulatory fervour about ensuring safety of women in public spaces does not take account the fact that most cases of sexual violence are perpetrated by persons known to the victim.

Resources and public imagination spent on such quick-fix solutions can be better used to improve public lighting, sensitise police, hire people in understaffed areas for policing etc. Technology cannot tackle a social problem. A multiplicity of approaches working together to address different aspects of the issue is required.

There is a greater need for critically assessing solutions pushed by the government in the name of women’s safety, as oftentimes, problematic policies find a convenient excuse in women’s safety. The Department of Telecommunications has made ‘panic buttons’ compulsory on all phones starting 1st January 2017. This has been mandated for even feature phones. Without a privacy legislation, this move could create a mechanism for law enforcement to track anyone with a phone in real-time, with an accuracy that was not possible with cell-tower data. No information has been disclosed about who would be the agency responsible for responding to alerts raised, and whether remote activation of GPS is possible. It therefore becomes hard to judge whether such a move is truly of any value.

So what features are on offer?

If you still think safety apps could work for you, you should pay attention to the different features on offer, and select what works best for you.

The thrust of these apps is that they leverage location information of the user in order to respond to situations of danger that the user may encounter. We grouped the main features under a few heads:

  1. Tracking upon alert (and additional information upon alert) The user is able to alert one or more parties when she finds herself in a dangerous situation. Usually, the information sent is the location of the user. In some apps, this information is supplemented with audio or video recordings taken during the time of alert, or some additional information in addition to location information.
  2. Constant tracking The user’s location information is trackable in real-time by one or more parties whom the user chooses. This type includes a very popular feature referred to as ‘Walk with me’, integrated in some google apps as well. It allows a remote contact to trace the user’s location constantly for a specified distance.
  3. Geofencing The user (or the ‘emergency contact’) selects an area on the map within which the user is expected to be. The user’s stepping outside of this area triggers the alert to the emergency contact.
  4. Heatmaps The user is alerted when a particular area is deemed unsafe. Such a determination is made on the basis of crowdsourced data on specific categories, or otherwise tagged data on different locations in the (usually) city.

Of the above features, the only one that does not involve being dependant on others for swift rescue is ‘Heatmaps’. But crowdsourced information about which areas can be perceived to be ‘safe’ might have class, caste and other biases that creep in, leading to an area being marked ‘unsafe’ for reasons that cannot be generalised. Safetipin, for example, avoids such biases to a large extent by having concrete parameters like lighting, openness on the basis of which users are encouraged to rate areas, along with some intangible factors like the feeling of safety that a place inspires.

Where do the alerts go?

Most apps we tested involve the user reacting to a dangerous situation and raising an alarm to alerts one or more actors. Depending on which app you choose, different actors would be involved in responding to the situation. We thought identifying the different types of actors to whom the alert is sent is another useful classification.

  1. Pre-determined contacts set by the user The app allows the user to select one or more contacts in her contacts list, and alerts are sent to these contacts. The contacts are notified when they are added as emergency contacts.
  2. Known third party The alerts are sent to a third party who is expected to respond to the situation, like police station control rooms or call centres of the app.
  3. Other ‘Networked publics’ The app sends alerts where more than one person can see it, like your Facebook wall. In other words, there is a logic behind where the alerts are sent, but there is no way of knowing who the alert reaches or does not reach. For example, one app sends an alert to users of the same app who are in the neighbourhood where the alert was raised.

Some apps send alerts to a combination of the above types of actors.

There are power relations at play between women using such apps and the different people they are able to alert in order to call for help. Depending on your personal circumstances, some actors would be less suited than others to respond to an emergency, so you need to see which one would work for you.

Alerting pre-determined contacts set by the user might at first seem to enhance user-choice, but it is very much possible that such contacts are husbands, brothers or other members of the family, who extend surveillance at homes to public spaces in the name of ‘protecting their women’.

Google recently announced its dedicated safety app called Trusted Contacts. The app doesn’t target women specifically. What's interesting however, is one of the features- when a trusted contact is worried about the user, he/she can request for the user's location. If the user happens to not respond (or not be able to respond), their location gets shared automatically. Such a feature could disproportionately affect women who are have several restrictions imposed on them by family members about what is a legitimate time, place and purpose to be out.

Are you “safe” when data about you is not safe?

At the time of downloading, all apps we assessed required permissions far greater than what the app really needs access to, in order to function. Information about alerts are sent via means that require numerous conditions fulfilled including having a reliable internet connection, through insecure means like SMS and social media posts.

32 out of 50 apps did not link to a privacy policy in the Play Store page. None of the government-offered safety apps except Jharkhand Police’s ‘Shakthi’ has a privacy policy. Where they were present, either on the Play Store listing or on the developer’s website, they were woefully lacking in information about what data they collect, whether they store, process and sell the data etc. In fact, the developers of one of the apps without a privacy policy offers analytics in e-commerce, retail, healthcare and government services sectors. This means that the developers are free to do as they please with the vast amounts of information gathered on users.

It might be an industry-wide practice to harvest personal data of users and sell it in a lucrative marketplace. Companies like Facebook have been reported to create micro-detailed profiles of users on the basis of their preferences, pages they visit etc. Why is it then surprising that safety apps are doing the same? It is especially ironic that apps claiming to enhance user safety treat sensitive personal information complacently.

In the absence of privacy protections in law and corporate responsibility, users of mobile phones are left with the imperfect solution of tweaking their own behaviour to reduce their data footprint. Location information being sensitive data, should be turned off after specific uses, say security gurus. In this context, safety apps encourage user behaviour to do the very opposite, as their very premise is that users should leave their location information on, in anticipation of danger, while making no commitment about keeping users’ data safe.

Accessibility of safety apps

We tested for three fields of accessibility for the visually impaired- whether the colour contrast of text and images is according to standards, whether all elements are labelled such that the app is readable by a screen-reader and whether touch targets are large enough or not. This assessment was done with the help of the ‘Accessibility Scanner’ app.

Sadly, none of the 50 apps were accessible with a screen reader. This adds visually impaired users to a long list of women who are excluded from the app-based solutions available. Women who do not own a smartphone, who cannot afford mobile data, who do not live in places with good connectivity, who cannot speak English- are all not suited to use safety apps. That leaves us with urban, able-bodied women who can afford smartphones and data packs. 

More information on how we went about the assessment:

We selected 50 apps that are representative of the types of solutions on offer. We included apps that are not developed specifically for Indian markets, but are claimed to be popular here. We also included one app that is not specifically marketed towards women, but has the maximum number of downloads and finds mention in articles that list the best women’s safety apps. We also chose some apps that have only 1000+ downloads, as they were interesting for other reasons like the profile of the developers, or exemplary of promoting stereotypes about women etc. You can find the table here.
