Gendering Surveillance
 
 
 
 
 

Production targets and surveillance targets

CCTV cameras in garment factories

Worldwide, closed circuit television (CCTV) cameras have become ubiquitous. On streets, at traffic lights, in public transport, apartment complexes, airports, classrooms, shops, workplaces etc., CCTV cameras are deployed for a variety of reasons. From preventing large crimes like terrorist attacks to minor infringements like littering or parking violations, CCTVs are one of the primary policing solutions. Even private places use CCTVs to monitor behaviour or ensure compliance. The benefits of deployment are celebrated- schools that have installed CCTV cameras note a decrease in rate of teacher beatings and thefts in the  classroom, apartment complexes that have installed CCTVs are able to help nab molesters captured in the act, in the absence of other incriminating evidence. Installation of CCTV cameras is a sureshot way to bring about a feeling of safety.

But large scale deployment of CCTVs has not gone without questioning. Some studies question the effectivity of CCTV cameras in curtailing crime and conclude that benefits do not outweigh the drawbacks. The government surveillance commissioner of the United Kingdom, whose capital has the highest density of CCTV cameras,, has discouraged deployment of CCTV cameras as they are ‘useless and ineffective’ in the absence of pointed information about what information is collected etc.

We started out to understand how technologies like CCTV cameras are used as tools to enable surveillance, but also safety in the lives of garment factory workers in Bangalore. Video surveillance for factories is a separate product category in itself.

The garment industry is a prime example of global manufacturing pressures impacting the working conditions of labourers in developing countries. The brands being transnational companies are able to shift production between developing countries depending on where it is cheapest to produce and where labour laws impose least amount of obligations. Under these competitive conditions, where a few big brands and retailers call the shots, factory units are not able to be equitable places for work.

We interviewed garment factory workers and conducted focus group discussions to explore the extent, nature and effect of surveillance on the behaviour of workers in environments where there is an asymmetry of power, production targets are high and conditions are exploitative. All the workers we spoke to worked in units of large factories that supplied to global brands. About 85% of the workers in factories are women.

Monitoring in factories of production targets is done by ‘supervisors’ who keep a check on the number of pieces in each part of the supply chain. In addition to monitoring the number of pieces, supervisors are also responsible for ensuring that the workers don’t waste time chatting with neighbours on the floor, or relax in any way leading to a waste of time. One worker tells us that if targets for the day are not met, then they do not get the day’s wages.

Views about CCTV cameras ranged from absolute opposition to ambivalence to hope that it is beneficial for the workers.

Even among workers who were confident that CCTV cameras are another tool of control, they qualified the remarks to say that it was acceptable to deploy cameras in others places beneficially.

                           

CCTVs are good in schools, on the road, in the public, in hospitals. But in factory it is torture. Even at the main gate it makes sense- so that they can check if anybody is stealing things. But on the floor in the batches, it becomes very hard for us to talk. If we want to talk about work related things like the union also it is hard.

Apart from the actions that the cameras intend to curtail, like wasting too much time going to the bathroom, or using mobile phones, the cameras also end up making normal private actions prone to watchfulness, making it highly uncomfortable for women.                            

Sometimes we scratch inside our blouses forgetting that there is a camera watching and suddenly it becomes very weird. We can’t scratch also.

All the workers we spoke to remember CCTV cameras having been there anywhere between 5 to 3 years back, depending on which factory or unit they work at. None of the workers we spoke to remember any advisory about why it was set up or who watches the feeds. However, knowledge about who watches the feeds and which rooms have the monitors where the feed plays has become common knowledge in a more informal way.

Asked how they know, the workers say that the supervisors and owners have told them that it is in the General Manager’s room. One worker from a smaller factory was punished for a reason which became evident from the cameras.

Me and my friend were called to the GM room for laughing. He saw it in the CCTV. They cut Rs. 5 for the day from our payment of Rs. 20. It was the first time something happened to me personally. Now we are scared to do anything- not even talk. We cannot rest also.

Workers in one of the largest factories report that there are CCTV cameras on the factory floor, in the lunch area, outside bathrooms and near the gates.

When inquired whether things have changed since CCTVs were installed, the workers lament that they are afraid they will be caught for minor infringements also more easily. The efficiency of watching everyone, as well as the promise of efficiency is threatening to the workers.

They could not catch us for small things before. Now they can see lots of us at once. You never know when they are watching. Things have definitely changed. We are so scared that we don’t even try to relax. There are times people get caught so we know that we are being watched. GM could not have watched so well without it. 

The workers did not have much to say when asked whether the cameras have been beneficial to them. While some workers see people getting caught for stealing needle bits and pieces as beneficial to them, others remarked that this is also ultimately beneficial for the owners and not for the workers.

There is the recognition that without access to footage and a say in means of control of the cameras, any reason given about its large-scale deployment is not of any use.

Only if we are shown the camera will it be useful for us. Even if there is something useful for us, they are capable of deleting it and keeping only what is good for them.

If supervisor scolds or tortures or harasses it can be useful. But we have not used it so far. We have used our phones to record videos and use that. That is probably why they don’t like us keeping phones inside.

In the protests against an amendment to the Employees Provident Funds and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1952, in April 2016, the police cooperated with the factory management to use public CCTV cameras to identify protesters from each factory. A fact finding report by the Karnataka People’s Union for Civil Liberties and Women Against Sexual Violence and State Repression- Karnataka confirms that CCTV footage was used in making arrests.

A culture of surveillance

The focus group discussions were conducted within a safe space, where the workers were willing to speak frankly about the issues they faced. But when interviewed around street corners and outside factory premises, some workers gave completely contrary accounts, despite being in the same factory unit. This was perhaps due to fear of retribution from the management and also the presence of their co-workers. In one instance, Girija, who was telling us about the use of CCTV cameras as a tool for threatening, turned around to deny the existence of CCTV cameras as soon as a co-worker joined us.

The work culture is such that employees are encouraged informally and by way of policies, to keep a check on their colleagues. Workers are divided into groups, and if in a group of 50 workers, 3 take a day off in a month, then the attendance bonus for all workers in the group gets cancelled. Workers are incentivised to keep each other accountable in such ways. The pressure to not take a day off is not only for their own attendance bonus, but also carry the responsibility of everybody else’s. The means of surveillance is not only through new technologies, but through social burdens of shared penalties.

Sexual harassment and CCTV cameras

Although justifications are not given before installing CCTV cameras, it is often brought up as one of the measures taken towards tackling the problem of widespread sexual harassment in factories. This is not surprising as ‘safety’ narratives are often used to increase surveillance mechanisms. One worker pointed out that CCTV cameras, contrary to popular belief, are detrimental to fights against sexual harassment. She was referencing an incident where there was an erasure of verbal sexual harassment, because the verbal abuse was not captured on camera, and seemed in the nature of ordinary exchange of words.

Technologies are only as beneficial as who controls them. There have been instances in one factory where a supervisor would ask one worker to come half an hour earlier than working hours, in order to solicit sexual favours and harass the worker. The supervisor would do so because of the knowledge that CCTV cameras would be turned on at the start of the work hours.

Further, when workers are vocal and protest against unfair conduct within the factory, they are asked to occupy places where the CCTV cameras are able to focus on them, both as a deterrent for such behaviour, and as punishment.
