1. The background

1.1. Thoughts on ‘CC cameras’

Opinions on ‘CC cameras’, a common way of referring to closed circuit television (CCTV) cameras amongst garment factory workers, are quite mixed. They range from absolute opposition to ambivalence to hope that it is beneficial for the workers.

Surveillance is usually multi-purposed and comes with a range of opportunities and disadvantages, which is why it is challenging to either support or reject it wholesale. (Rosenblat, Kleese, boyd 2014)

Gowri, a factory worker from the Peenya area, was confident that CCTV cameras are yet another tool of control in the factory. However, she made sure to qualify her remarks to say that it was beneficial to have such cameras elsewhere, like in public places.

CCTVs are good in schools or on the road or in hospitals. But in the factory, it is torture. Even at the main gate it makes sense - so that they can check if anybody is stealing things. But on the floor in the batches, it becomes very hard for us to talk. If we want to talk about work related things like the union also it is hard.

Still others, like Girija, who has worked in different garment factories for the last 17 years, defended CCTV cameras staunchly. She advances several ways in which they are beneficial for women working in factories:

CC cameras are good and should be there in all factories. Women also benefit from it sometimes. There is theft, teasing, harassment during which CC cameras come of use.

Lots of women sneak in mobile phones and constantly want to talk. How can you work if you talk so much? It is not only for emergency that they talk. Sometimes they go to the bathroom and talk to boys and then get raped. With CC cameras, these things reduce.

To go out, you need a permission slip. If something goes wrong while the women take permission slip and step out, then factories are blamed for it. So factories are correct in taking measures.

Girija’s views on CCTV cameras are reminiscent of CCTV evangelism that is common in the news about women’s safety: seeking to tackle multiple kinds of problems, with a large helping of moral panic. (PTI 2015) She also considered the theft of needle bits and cloth pieces not simply as a problem of her employer, but the tackling of which she also had a personal stake in.

An employee who objects to the notion that they are being viewed as a potential thief by their employer may accept and look forward to the possibility that their hard work will be rendered visible to upper management.’ (Rosenblat, Kleese, boyd 2014)

It was clear from the get-go that among the garment factory workers, the idea of safety through surveillance had gained a foothold, in varying degrees.

1.2. Why garment factory workers?

The garment industry is a prime example of global manufacturing pressures impacting the working conditions of labourers in developing countries. (Delahanty 1999) Big clothing brands being transnational companies, are able to shift production between developing countries depending on where it is cheapest to produce and where labour laws impose the least amount of obligations. (Delahanty 1999)

In these competitive conditions, garment factories which supply to these big brands are under immense pressure to extract maximum work from their employees while expending minimum costs - not the best formula for equitable workplaces.

Forcing a less than adequate workforce number to work overtime with no extra pay and harassment of women workers by male supervisors have become entrenched as “unavoidable” ways of meeting deadlines. And using deadlines as an excuse, a culture of fear and powerlessness has been cultivated on the shop floor, to prevent workers from articulating even basic rights — be it wages, or time to have lunch or take leave. (Ratnam 2007)

The garments industry in India being more than 85% constituted of women (Ravi 2015), presents a unique opportunity to understand the role that technologies like CCTV cameras play in the monitoring of employees, but also in the increasing demands of workers, for measures to be taken against sexual harassment in the workplace.

We interviewed key informants from Cividep, Garment Labour Union and other organisations working on labour rights of garment factory workers in Bangalore. We also conducted interviews and focus group discussions with women and men workers in the areas of Peenya and Mysore Road. All names used in the article are fictitious, to protect the identity of the interviewees.

2. Sexual Harassment or Why CCTV Cameras Might Be A Good Thing

2.1. No formal justifications for CCTV cameras

None of the workers remember any advisory or justifications being given before CCTV cameras were installed in their workplace. There was no expectation of such communication from the workers.

Why do they have to give us reasons? No reasons were given.

Over time, CCTV cameras have become normalised in factory environments.

It was there before I joined. Cameras were there in the old factory I worked at also.

Some estimates from the workers about when CCTV cameras were first installed date back to 2008, following the suicide of Ammu, a garment worker who killed herself in a factory bathroom, on account of unbearable harassment from factory authorities, according to the suicide note in her palm. (Ratnam 2007) Workers say that installation of CCTV cameras were one of the measures taken to be seen responding to widespread protests within the garments industry sector about deplorable working conditions and increasing factory-related deaths (there were 3 in 2007 alone). (McVeigh 2007)

When asked if Rupa can hazard a guess why CCTV cameras were installed, she offers a reason that came up repeatedly in conversations with workers across factories.

 

What is the use for us of CC cameras? It has been there for 5 years, since 5 years no one has given a reason. We have heard from others that it is due to theft. Of course the company will want it, it is very useful for them to instill fear in us.

One of the larger factories that supplies to big brands has CCTV cameras installed on the factory floor, in the lunch area, outside bathrooms and near the gates. This is not the case in all factories, however. Workers in smaller factories report that cameras are placed only near the gates and on the production floor.

2.2. Measures to tackle sexual harassment include CCTV cameras

The extent of sexual harassment in garment factories is detailed in a June, 2016 report by Sisters for Change and Munnade. The report notes that about 60 percent of women garment workers faced intimidation and violence in workplaces.

After a lot of pressure from labour unions, and rights groups like Cividep, on global brands to keep a tab on basic working conditions in their supply chains, multi-stakeholder roundtables that meet periodically have been constituted to tackle sexual harassment.

In these fora, CCTV cameras have often been brought up as one of the measures taken to tackle the problem of widespread sexual harassment in factories, says Usha, a labour union activist we spoke to.

Usha has experience working with garment factory workers for many years. She points out that CCTV cameras, contrary to popular belief, can sometimes be detrimental to fights against sexual harassment.

In one case, a complaint of repeated sexual harassment by the supervisor was not taken seriously, thanks to CCTV cameras. The supervisor would come and say lewd things to the worker on the factory floor and provoke her in all kinds of ways, but because the cameras are not able to capture the sound of his words, the exchange did not appear out of the ordinary, and the complaint was dismissed. 

Erasure of verbal forms of sexual harassment is a pattern that has been noticed in the context of video surveillance elsewhere too. (Koskela 2000, 2002)

Such an erasure of verbal sexual harassment is instructive that CCTV cameras, like any technology, can work in positive or negative ways. Whether it is beneficial or not is determined by details like who has ownership over the device and data, and who has information about its operational details.

Claims that CCTV cameras are setup to also aid workers in reducing workplace and sexual harassment holds no water if control over the systems lies squarely with the management. Perhaps, this is why many workers are reminded of their phones as more reliable tools in holding supervisors and others accountable for harassment.

2.3. Not the first time safety used as an excuse for surveillance

Safety discourses and restrictions are often hard to tell apart. This is best demonstrated in the movement against restrictive hostel rules for women in Delhi University, called Pinjra Tod (break the cage).

A member of Pinjra Tod, who remained anonymous in the article talks about how CCTV cameras were being used in her paying guest (PG) accommodation for the sake of moral policing. (Malu 2016)

In my PG we had CCTV cameras to record our movements, entry and exit timings, rules to not wear short clothes, take wardens written permission to go out or stay out at night, parents approval for trivial things and constantly proving our ‘good girl’ reputation to maintain the elitist image of these accommodation.

CCTV cameras are very popular solutions for authorities forging ‘women’s safety’ agendas. At great costs to their movements and privacy, CCTV cameras are installed in women’s living and working environments in the name of their safety. (IANS 2014)

2.4. CCTV cameras not an effective deterrent

Effectiveness of video surveillance systems as a deterrent for violence against women in public places as well as within private establishments has been inconclusive, yet it remains to be pushed without a discussion about the particulars.

There are instances of extreme forms of violence against women that have taken place in the workplace in the presence of CCTV cameras. The rape of an employee of Infosys in Pune is one recent example. News reports on the incident say that company officials were ‘flabbergasted’. The company has an Infosys Emergency app, panic buttons on all buses and cabs and CCTV cameras on its premises. (Express News Service 2017)

“Yesterday’s unfortunate incident is a reminder however that nothing is foolproof, and we are continuing to seek recommendations and suggestions from different stakeholders on how we can try and strengthen the safety of our employees...” read the official statement from the company, whose security personnel was the perpetrator. (Express News Service 2017)

CCTV cameras’ role in deterrence of crime even in public places has been questioned time and again. (Schneier 2008; Doctorow 2011)

In a recent incident that took place in Bangalore, a molester was nabbed, due in part to footage obtained from a CCTV camera. (Express News Service 2017) This has led to an evangelical cry to get all private establishments, especially apartment complexes to install CCTV cameras with renewed fervour.

2.4. New complications and subversions

There are plenty of cases than where CCTV cameras have enabled new forms of harassment and avenues of voyeurism. (The Wire Staff 2017; Rashid 2015)

Aruna, who heads an NGO that works with garment workers, notes another incident in a different factory, where a supervisor would ask a worker to come half an hour earlier than working hours, in order to solicit sexual favours. The supervisor would do so emboldened by the knowledge that CCTV cameras would be turned on only at the start of the work hours.

This is an illustration of what is called “displacement effect”, (Journalists Resource) which pushes the forbidden activity to parts not covered by the surveillance. It is a which comes up in studies on public surveillance systems, where spatial displacements of crime to other parts of the city are common. However, the displacement effect is equally relevant in CCTV deployment in private establishments like the case in point, in the form of a temporal displacement made by the person who is aware of the particulars of operation of the cameras.

3. Set in an already exploitative industry

Addition of CCTV cameras in the mix of massive asymmetries of power, without sharing any control over these systems, is only likely to heighten exploitation.

3.1. Monitoring has always existed in factories

Monitoring of production targets in these factories is done by ‘supervisors’, for every process in the supply chain. If targets for the day are not met, then the worker does not get the day’s wages. In addition to ensuring that production targets are met, supervisors are also responsible for making sure that workers don’t waste time chatting with neighbours on the floor, or relax in any way leading to a decrease in efficiency.

We cannot talk at all. You tell me- do you think you can spend a whole day without talking? That’s what they make us do.

Wasting time chatting is, however, something that the management intends to curtail: along with wasting too much time going to the bathroom, using mobile phones, unionising[a] etc. (Ratnam 2007)

But CCTV cameras also end up making normal, private actions prone to watchfulness, leading to discomfort for women workers.

Sometimes we scratch inside our blouses forgetting that there is a camera watching and suddenly it becomes very weird. Nowadays I try to ignore any itch.

To assess the value of any technology, the context of power relations in which it is set is very important.

3.2. Political economy in which factories operate

Garment factories in India compete with counterparts in neighbouring countries like Bangladesh and Laos to supply cheap labour for manufacturing. The demographic of workers being largely female, young, migrant and desperately poor, is no coincidence as the garment industry finds the intersection the easiest to exploit. (Ratnam 2007)

It is no surprise then that new technologies are being used to increase efficiency and intrusiveness of monitoring.

They monitor how much time we are in the bathroom. There are watchmen near the bathroom and if we are there for too long in the stall, they will knock. We go there to use our phones but sometimes we get caught. Going to bathroom is monitored from the cameras also.

‘Factory surveillance systems’ is a separate product category offered by companies offering security solutions. Garment factories of all levels in Bangalore use video surveillance in their premises. In an industry where a slight increase in wages or a slight decrease in productivity means production is lost to neighbouring countries with cheaper labour, CCTV cameras are crucial for the management.

3.3. Security theater

Video surveillance does the job of ‘security theater’ more than that of security. One worker notes that it is not as if thefts have completely stopped. A raid by the factory owner into a worker’s house revealed about 400 pieces of sleeves having been stolen over a period of time. It wasn’t surveillance cameras that helped catch the worker, but a tip-off from a jealous co-worker.

When asked working conditions have changed since CCTVs were installed, the workers lament that they have become more afraid that they will be caught for minor infringements more easily. The efficiency of watching everyone, as well as the promise of efficiency is threatening.

They could not catch us for small things before. Now they can see lots of us at once. We never know when they are watching. Things have definitely changed. We are so scared that we don’t even try to relax. There are times people get caught so we know that we are being watched. GM could not have watched so well without it.

 

‘Employers have a strong incentive to maintain control and order of their workplaces, and surveillance is often used to achieve this goal. CCTV cameras are usually visible in the workplace (although Nanny cams are a prominent exception), as a signal to workers (and sometimes customers) that they are being watched so that they behave accordingly. Much like cameras were intended to communicate surveillance to Yorkshire’s prostitutes by recording the details of their location and activities, cameras are a warning that a record of bad behavior is available to the cameras’ operators.’ (Rosenblat, Kneese, boyd 2014)

4. CCTV cameras and Freedom of Assembly and Association

CCTVs are used to crackdown on workers who are vocal and and protest against unfair conduct. Hema, a new member of the union, but a long-time ‘loudmouth’ by her own admission, stood up for her friend when the supervisor spoke to her brashly for not being able to show up for ‘over-time’ (OT) work. She has since been made to occupy a place where the CCTV cameras are able to focus on her, both as punishment and as a deterrent for such behaviour.

I feel really bad that no one talks to me. I am asked to sit far away from the others. They turn the cameras to point at me so that they can keep me under observation and make sure that I don’t have anybody to talk to.

It is very common for women who talk back in factories to be vindictively targeted with surveillance cameras, and isolate them from their co-workers, says Malathi, a labour union activist and former garments worker.

In the protests against an amendment to the Employees Provident Funds and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1952, in April 2016, the police cooperated with the factory management to use public CCTV cameras to identify protesters from each factory. Data from public surveillance cameras installed at traffic signals were shared with factory owners to zero down on ‘troublemakers’. A fact finding report by the Karnataka People’s Union for Civil Liberties and Women Against Sexual Violence and State Repression- Karnataka confirms that CCTV footage was used in making arrests.

It’s a shocking instance of the police being in cohorts with big capital, and using public CCTV cameras to undermine civil and political rights of workers. Ever since that incident, workers do not linger around outside the gates, says Malathi.

5. Examining power relations

Benefits of CCTV deployment in private establishments are celebrated: schools that have installed CCTV cameras note a decrease in rate of teacher beatings and thefts in the classroom (MPost 2015), apartment complexes that have installed CCTVs are able to help nab molesters in the absence of other incriminating evidence. (Express News Service 2017)

Success stories are exported to legitimise video surveillance in other contexts without going into the details of who watches the footage, how the data is handled, and what could be harms that have been overlooked. If the balance of power between the surveilling and the surveilled does not shift for the better, CCTV cameras can in fact amplify inequality, like in the case of garment factory workers, where control over the devices and data lies squarely with one party.

Studies of context and use discrimination highlight the valence toward unequal power relations with the introduction of new technologies. (Monahan 2009) Because technologies are underdetermined, meaning that they take on values from the context of their use, existing conditions of inequality inflect technologies and technological systems, reproducing unequal social orders (Monahan 2005).

5.1. Who controls the footage

When asked whether workers are aware who monitors the feeds, workers say that their supervisors have told them that it is in the General Manager’s (GM) room.

There is the recognition among workers that without access to footage and a say in means of control of the cameras, any reason given about its large-scale deployment is not of any use.

Only if we are shown the camera will it be useful for us. Even if there is something useful for us, they are capable of deleting it and keeping only what is good for them. If supervisor scolds or tortures or harasses it can be useful. But we have not used it so far. We have used our phones to record videos and use that. That is probably why they don’t like us keeping phones inside.

Mobile phones have been proven to be very powerful in the hands of workers. Many factories require workers to leave mobile phones behind in the lockers before getting to work, although it is common practice for women to sneak it in by putting it in their blouses. One of the purposes the phone can serve, is what CCTV cameras can do for the management: help in capturing evidence against the other party.

CCTV cameras are not undesirable, by definition. As stressed in earlier sections, power relations need to be examined to assess the value to any technology.

5.2. What the management does with the footage

Workers have faced consequences for being caught on camera doing things unpalatable to the management. One worker from a smaller factory was punished for a reason which became evident from a CCTV camera.

Me and my friend were called to the GM room for laughing. He saw it in the CC camera. They cut Rs. 5 for the day from our payment of Rs. 20. It was the first time something happened to me personally. Now we are scared to do anything - not even talk. We cannot rest also.

Workers were never formally informed about the purpose(s) the CCTV cameras would serve. Supervisors use the camera to warn again stealing, just as well as to warn against wasting time. Without a purpose limitation, the ‘function creep’ of the technology enables its use in more insidious ways than what might have initially been intended.

CCTV cameras might very well provide heretofore non-existent tools for safety and security in the hands of workers in factories. But that is contingent on workers having an understanding of the technologies, their terms of use in the factory i.e., the timings it is operable during, access to footage, a say in how long the data may be retained for and by whom, among others.

6. A culture of surveillance

The work culture is such that employees are encouraged informally and by way of policies, to keep a check on their colleagues. For example, in one factory, workers are divided into groups, and if in a group of 50 workers, if 3 take a day off in a month, then the attendance bonus for all workers in the group gets cancelled. Workers are incentivised to keep each other answerable in such ways. The pressure to not take a day off is not only for their own attendance bonus, but also carry the responsibility of everybody else’s. The means of surveillance is not only through new technologies, but through social burdens of shared penalties.

The focus group discussions were conducted within a safe space, where the workers were willing to speak frankly about the issues they faced. Even when approached near the streets leading to their factories at the end of the work day, or in public view during street plays, workers were happy to open up about their opinions on CCTV cameras. However, presence of co-workers during the conversation meant that their views would turn agreeable with the management, even if it contradicted what they were saying minutes before, prior to the co-worker joining us. This was perhaps due to fear of being identified and facing retribution from the management.

7. In conclusion

There is no going back when it comes to workplace surveillance in private establishments. Technologies that are far more advanced than CCTV cameras are being developed to measure and enhance efficiency. (Rosenblat, Kleese, boyd 2014) Such technologies need not always spell doom for employees either, even though they are aimed at disciplining and bringing about conformity in workforces.

New technologies deployed by employers in exploitative industries still does not get the kind of attention from grassroot labour rights movements that it deserves. The National People’s Tribunal for living wages and decent working conditions of garment workers was held in its third edition in 2011, when one of the recommendations made to the jury was to consider the effects of the practice of affixing CCTV cameras to monitor workers in factories. This recommendation was not included in the final jury verdict. (Clean Clothes Campaign 2012)

Without enough attention given to the control over devices and the data, technologies like CCTV cameras get normalised without a chance for the surveilled asserting agency over them.
